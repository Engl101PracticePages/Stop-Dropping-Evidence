1) Mini lesson: evidence is not analysis

The problem: Students use a quote/paraphrase and then their next sentence just restates the same information. That is repetition, not analysis.
The fix: After any quote/paraphrase, write 3 sentences:
  1. This means… (translate the evidence)
  2. This matters because… (so what?)
  3. This connects to my claim by… (link back)

Example (nursing)

Evidence (paraphrase + placeholder citation)

The Bureau of Labor Statistics describes registered nurses as providing and coordinating patient care, educating patients, and administering treatments. (BLS)

Analysis (3-sentence rule)

This means the role involves both medical tasks and communication responsibilities, not just “helping people.”
This matters because the daily work includes emotional labor and high responsibility, which can affect stress and job satisfaction.
This connects to my claim by showing that whether nursing is a “good fit” depends on the kind of work a person wants to do, not only job growth.

Note: you are allowed to say a career is “rewarding” or “great,” but you need evidence and explanation. You can’t just drop the word and move on.

2) Practice: repetition vs. analysis (with the evidence included)

Directions: Each item includes Evidence (what a student might quote/paraphrase) and one After-evidence sentence. Label the after-evidence sentence: R repetition (restates the evidence) • A analysis (explains meaning, importance, connection, limitation, etc.)
Answer key (quick why):
  1. R — repeats the education point, no “so what.”
  2. A — interprets what that duty suggests about skills/labor.
  3. R — repeats growth, no meaning/limits.
  4. A — adds implication + limitation (growth ≠ guaranteed comfort).
  5. R — restates “many settings.”
  6. A — connects flexibility to a claim about fit.
  7. R — repeats physical demands, no interpretation.
  8. A — interprets the tradeoff and links back to a claim.

3) Revise your draft analysis (use your actual draft + your actual in-text citation)

What to do:
  1. Find a place in your draft where you used evidence (quote or paraphrase) and then wrote an “analysis” sentence.
  2. Copy/paste that excerpt below (keep your in-text citation exactly as you wrote it).
  3. Pull out your evidence sentence and your analysis sentence(s).
  4. Rewrite the analysis using the 3-sentence rule.
  5. Choose one analysis move to go deeper.

A) Paste the original excerpt from your draft (3–6 sentences)

Either quote or paraphrase is fine. Keep your in-text citation exactly as written.

B) Evidence sentence (from your draft)

Example format: ... (BLS) or ... (“Registered Nurses”). Use what you already used in your draft.

C) Your original analysis sentence(s) (from your draft)

This is the part that often turns into repetition. We’re rewriting it.

D) Your claim (optional but helpful)

If you include your claim, your “connects” sentence will be easier to write.

E) Rewrite your analysis using the 3-sentence rule (required)

F) Choose 1 “analysis move” (small Option C)

Pick one move and add one more sentence. This is the “go deeper” part.

Tip: your “analysis” should add meaning, not just repeat the evidence.

Common mistake to avoid: If your “This means…” sentence is basically the evidence with synonyms, it’s still repetition. Make it interpretive: what does the evidence suggest, imply, or complicate?

4) Self-check before you submit

Reminder: if your “analysis” sentence could be replaced with “This shows ___” and nothing changes, it’s probably repetition.
